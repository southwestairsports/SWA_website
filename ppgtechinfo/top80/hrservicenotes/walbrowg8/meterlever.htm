Fuel Metering Lever on the WG-8 carburetor

by Had Robinson

The down movement of the metering lever diaphragm is limited by an outer metal skirt that surrounds the center button on the diaphragm.  If the metering lever gets bent, the carburetor can starve the engine of fuel under high load conditions.  This is because the free travel of the diaphragm is restricted by design and it cannot push the bent lever down far enough to deliver enough fuel at higher loads.  The engine may run at partial loads but when WOT (wide open throttle) is engaged, it may sputter or die.

Owners of the WG-8 must be careful when priming the carburetor.  It is better not to press on the diaphragm at all unless it is equipped with the special priming lever now supplied on all Top 80 carburetors.  If your carburetor does not have this special accessory to prime it, then it is safer to prime it the old fashioned way: crank the motor with full choke until the fuel pump gets fuel into the carburetor.  Better, first remove the fuel line at the carburetor and pressurize the fuel tank by blowing on the priming tube.  This will fill the fuel lines with fuel.  Quickly reattach the fuel line to the carburetor while holding some pressure in the fuel tank.  Another option is to purchase the Miniplane priming lever kit from us.
To see if you bent your metering lever, remove the cover plate and measure the distance from the top of the carburetor housing to the top of the fuel metering lever (where it contacts the button on the diaphragm).  This distance should be 1.7mm (the height that all carburetors have when new from the factory).

To verify that this is the correct height, I opened up a WG-8 on a brand new Top 80 and measured the metering lever height.  It was 1.7mm.  This also agrees with the preset height of the metering lever supplied in the Walbro rebuild kits for the WG-8.

However, the official Miniplane service manual states that it should be 0.5mm - 0.7mm.  Why would a new WG-8 come from the Miniplane factory with a metering lever height of 1.7mm when it is supposed to be 1mm less?

Our experience has been that the difference between 0.7mm and 1.7mm is not that important.  The diaphragm has a great range of movement and its function would only be affected if the metering lever height is significantly outside specifications.  The surface area of the diaphragm does not change, regardless of the metering lever height.  (The tech note from someone at Walbro failed to mention this important fact.)  The critical value is the pop-off spring tension because this and the main jet are what determine the fuel/air ratio that enters the engine.  If you increase the height more than 1.7mm (by forcing the metering lever diaphragm down with some tool), the diaphragm will bottom out on the carburetor housing and starve the engine at high loads.  If the metering lever is much lower than 0.7mm, it will force the inlet needle valve open even when the engine is off and flood it.

In conclusion, it is safer for the engine to have a lesser than a greater height because there is less chance of it running too lean.  I am continuing to experiment with all of this and will post any changes here.
