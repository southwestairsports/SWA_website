Propeller hub removal and installation

There may be times when the propeller hub will not easily come off the shaft.  Whatever you do, DO NOT HIT IT WITH A HAMMER or PRY IT OFF!  (That is, if you do not want to have to rebuild the redrive.

Because the hub is aluminum and shaft is hardened steel, it is an easy job to do but only if you have the right tools.  You will need an ordinary gear puller and a propane torch.  WD-40 should be used to help soften things up for removal.

1.  If it is a particularly corroded hub, apply liberal amounts of WD-40 and let the whole thing sit overnight.

2. Attach the gear puller as in the photo below and apply about 15 Nm of torque on the gear puller.  Keep the center screw in the shaft as a convenient and safe place for the gear puller to exert its force.  If you are lucky, it might come right off.  If it does not, do not apply more force to the puller. 

3. Use the torch at full power for about 30 seconds to heat the part of the hub near the shaft.  Be very careful to to not get the flame near the base of the propeller shaft where the neoprene seal is.  As the hub heats up, you may hear the aluminum expanding -- and breaking away from the shaft.  Be patient.  When you hear it go "clink" three or four times, apply force on the gear puller again.  If you did it right, the hub should start to move.  But as it cools, it may grip the shaft again you will have to apply more heat.  DO NOT OVERHEAT THE HUB!  It should not take more than 30-50 seconds of heat before it breaks free.  If you see the slightest bit of smoke, STOP IMMEDIATELY and let things cool down.

4. To reinstall the hub, heat it with the torch full power for 15 seconds and then immediately put it on the shaft and drive it in with a dead-blow hammer.  The hub has a tight fit and must be installed with heat.  If you do not get if fully seated, it will eventually loosen and the steel shaft will destroy the aluminum hub.

The redrive here had been used in an ocean environment and the hub was badly corroded and firmly seized to the shaft.  Proper application of heat allowed it to slip right off.
