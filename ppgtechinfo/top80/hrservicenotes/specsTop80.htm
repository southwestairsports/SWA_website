Top 80 specifications

Compiled by Had Robinson from many sources including our shop

General

Exploded parts diagrams engine  redrive  redrive before 2004  exhaust  starter  carburetor (go to Mecafly for parts identification)

Carburetor main jet – size 116 (sea level).  We have the high altitude (HA) jets.  Contact us to purchase. 
Carburetor metering lever height – 0.5mm - 0.7mm.  Too high a value will lean out the engine at high loads and may ruin it!
Carburetor pop-off pressure – see "Pop-off pressure" below.
Carburetor primer lever depth below top of diaphragm cover < 4.0mm
Clutch bell housing minimum thickness 1.2mm
Clutch springs – overall length 31mm.  If more than this, they must be replaced.  The clutch should engage at 2,500 - 3,500 RPM.
Coil resistance - 8.5K Ohms +-10% (measure with a needle stuck into the secondary wire right where it comes out of the coil.)
Coil + secondary wire resistance – 17K +-10% Ohms.  If the secondary wire has been replaced with our kit, the value will be 8.8K Ohms +-10%
Coil to flywheel gap – 0.38mm (0.015").  IDM specs it 0.35mm - 0.45mm. Miniplane specs it 0.30 mm but new engines are at 0.38 mm.
Compression – about 150 psi (sea level); 135 psi (4,000' MSL) Do NOT put any oil in the cylinder before testing!
Crankshaft – longer end is the flywheel side
Cylinder head temperature – max 200°C, cruise temp 120°C - 150°C.  Temperatures above 160°C should be for brief periods only.  Actual temperatures
     will depend on ambient conditions and altitude.  Engines run at seal level will experience greater operating temperatures.
Cylinder squish – 0.6mm-0.7mm (0.024"-0.028")  For low octane fuel make the thickness 0.80mm-0.95mm.  Engines before June 2003
     require 0.80mm-0.85mm.  Note: New gaskets compress approx. 25%.  This must be noted when calculating the gasket size.  Most common
     sizes are .30mm & .40mm.  Excessive gasket thickness is ALWAYS better than too thin!  Too thin a gasket can cause engine damage.
Engine mounting screws – the screws on the harness side (M6x8) are shorter than on the engine side (M6x10).  Use blue threadlock!
Exhaust flange springs/nuts – see this page for notes and how to tighten.
Flywheel diameter – 90mm
Fuel – aviation gasoline (AVGAS) or premium grade unleaded gasoline, ethanol free.  For more info, see the fuel-oil specification page.
Redrive oil – see "Redrive oil" below
Fuel filter – See this page
Fuel line size – carburetor to filter ID 1/8" x OD 3/16"; filter to fuel tank ID 3/16" x OD 5/16" – use Excelon fuel line or equivalent
Fuel pump vacuum – 5" Hg (dry) 7" Hg (wet w/ fuel)
Ignition coil gap – see "Coil to flywheel gap" above
Ignition timing – Official value is 14.5° BTDC (0.90mm - 0.95mm/0.0354" - 0.0374") piston BTDC.  Check timing  Change timing
Main bearing – SKF BB1B 447205A
Main seal 18mm x 28mm x 7mm FP
Muffler springs – Use paraglider line to stretch the springs for replacement/removal.  Do NOT use pliers to stretch the springs because
    this will nick the tempered surface of the spring and weaken it.  Here is a video on how to properly do it
Oil – 2% mix requires high quality 2 stroke 100% synthetic or semi-synthetic oil.  For more info, see our fuel-oil specification page.
Pop-off spring pressure – 1.2-1.3 Bar (17.5-19.0 psi).  Rest pressure must be 10 psi or greater.
Redrive clutch bell housing minimum thickness – see "Clutch bell housing minimum thickness" above
Redrive clutch springs – see "Clutch springs" above
Redrive oil – Miniplane specifies 50 ml of SAE 80W-140 gear oil.  75W-90 fully synthetic gear oil may also be used.
Redrive fill plug relief pressure – 1.5-2.0 psi.  If this relief valve becomes clogged, the redrive will leak oil.
Safety net dimensions – these are needed if repairs are made to the safety netting or if pilots want to make their own.
Secondary wire + coil resistance – see "Coil + secondary wire resistance" above
Secondary wire resistance (only) – 8.5K Ohms +-10%  As the resistance of the wire is measured closer to the coil, this value will decrease
     proportionately.  The secondary wire has a resistance of about 570 Ohms/inch (225 Ohms/cm).  If the wire from our secondary replacement kit
     is used, the resistance is about 13 Ohms/inch (5.1 Ohms/cm).
Spark plug gap 0.5mm-0.6 mm. (.020” - .024”) If the gap is excessive, the engine will not reach full power.
Spark plug info – Here is detailed info on the spark plug and how it works in the Top 80.
Spark plug type – NGK B9ES or B9EG (cold flying conditions) or NGK B10ES or B10EG (hot flying conditions).  Do not use resistor type plugs unless you
     have replaced the secondary wire with our kit and notice interference in your 2 way radio.  The correct heat range for the plug is necessary to
     prevent fouling or overheating.  Always use red threadlock on the terminal cap lug.
Squish – See "Cylinder squish" above
Starter rope – 1.25m x 3.0mm Dyneema.  Miniplane-USA has this superior cord over the stock OEM cord.  It will last years.

Torque Values

         A. Torque conversion chart (Newton meters to/from inch pounds)

        B. Torque Values – here is a complete chart of the proper torque values for bolts into steel and aluminum.  Reduce all torque value by 50%
             with screws made ​​of aluminum or short threads in aluminum, reduce torque by 60% with screws in plastic.

        C. WARNING: Socket button-head screws and studs CANNOT be torqued like ordinary bolts.  Torque value is about 25% of the standard value.
            The appropriate threadlock must always be used on these types of fasteners.

        D. Threadlock – There are (2) types of threadlock: "blue" for intermediate strength and "red" for high strength.  Most hardware requires the blue.
             Red is much stronger and hardware put together with red cannot be removed without heat.  It is important that studs inserted
             into the engine case be coated with red as the extreme vibration and shear loads will quickly loosen them.

            USE COPIOUS AMOUNTS OF BRAKE CLEANER AND COMPRESSED AIR TO CLEAN ALL SURFACES ASSEMBLED WITH THREADLOCK.

E. Engines running near or at sea level run hotter and have greater output which causes things to loosen, especially the cylinder head assembly.

Carburetor mounting screws - 4 Nm.  It is easy to strip the threads in the crankcase.  Use of threadlock is not necessary.
Clutch nut 38 - 40 Nm use of threadlock is not necessary
Cooling box to crankcase (4) bolts 10 Nm -- threadlock is optional on these bolts
Cooling fan/starter to flywheel screws -- see "Finger screws" below.
Cylinder head nuts 9 Nm  Tighten in a cross pattern to 4 Nm and then to 9 Nm.  Note: do not use thread locking compounds on these nuts.
     Order:  Short bolt - washer, nut; Long bolt - spacer, washer, nut.  After engine assembly, put the large metal washer on the stud,
     then the cooling air duct, plastic washer, and locknut.  Failure to get the order correctly will damage the cowling.  The locknuts holding
     the air duct should be just snug.  If they are torqued down the same as the nuts beneath, they will damage the air duct.
     DO NOT USE A CHEAP 1/4" CLICK-TYPE TORQUE WRENCH.  USE A 1/4" BEAM-TYPE TORQUE WRENCH.
Engine mounting button-head screws 3-4 Nm for M6; 5 Nm for M8.  Blue threadlock keeps them from loosening.
Exhaust button-head screws – 5 Nm Install these AFTER the exhaust flange nuts have been tightened
Exhaust flange springs/nuts – see this page for notes and how to tighten.
Exhaust flange studs – 2 Nm.  Use red (high strength) threadlock on these studs or they will loosen.
Finger screws – 10 Nm  A 10mm deep socket must be used to tighten these screws!  Threadlock is optional.
Flywheel nut 38 - 40 Nm  Temporary torque for setting timing 2 Nm.  Do NOT use threadlock on this nut.

M4 bolts 2.5 - 3 Nm
M5 bolts 4 - 5 Nm
M6 bolts 9 - 11 NM
M8 bolts 20 - 24 Nm

Muffler mounting button head screws – 8 Nm  Note: blue threadlock must be used on these screws.
Muffler rubber mounts (in engine) – Tighten firmly with adjustable pliers that grip the steel washer on the side that has the stud.  Use blue
     threadlock on the stud -- this is very important!
Prop bolts 6-10 Nm – It is better to be on the loose side when tightening. Note: failure to keep these torqued properly can destroy the hub.
     Do not over-tighten!  Wooden propellers are particularly prone to loosening and must be checked regularly.  Replace the locking nuts
     when they turn easily or use blue threadlock.  Make sure the rubber washer is under the outer flange and NOT between the propeller
     and the hub/spacer.
Prop hub center bolt 9-10 NM – it is important to use blue threadlock on this bolt.
Redrive nuts - 20 - 24 Nm  When assembling be certain to check the clutch for drag before torqueing the nuts down.
Redrive fill & drain plugs - 5-6 Nm.  Note: drain/fill plugs are not designed for high torque values.  It is easy to strip them.
     Never use thread locking compound on drain/fill plugs.
Reed valve screws - 4-5 Nm Use blue threadlock on these screws.
Spark Plug – 20-30 Nm.  If you do not have a torque wrench and the plug is new, first hand tighten
     then tighten with a wrench an additional 90-120 degrees. Note: if there is a CHT installed beneath the plug, this technique will not work.
     Use of anti-seize compound is optional.  Be sure to use red threadlock on the spark plug terminal or it WILL loosen.

Replacement/Check Intervals (from Miniplane's maintenance website and other sources)

General instructions

A. If the ignition and fuel systems are in order, engine performance is directly related to engine compression.  If the compression is around 150 psi (at sea level), the engine top end is in good order.  If this is the case, replacing parts like the piston or piston ring is unnecessary.  Use of the best fuel (AVGAS) and oil (full-synthetic or semi-synthetic with the JASO FD and API TC classifications) will give your Top 80 the longest life before a major overhaul is necessary.  Use of gasoline with ethanol will increase engine maintenance and cause rapid deterioration of fuel system parts.  It does not mix as well with fully synthetic oils.  If you have to use ethanol fuels, use a semi-synthetic oils.

B. Maintenance schedules depend on how the engine is used.  Thankfully, the engine is just a few inches from the pilot's head so he can monitor it closely.  Any growing ping type noise (the rattling of loose bearings) must be carefully noted as destruction of the upper piston rod bearing is a disaster.  Top end failure might be an issue after 500 hours or more but few pilots put this amount of hours on their engines over a lifetime.

C. Abused engines and those that experience fuel starvation can experience top end failure very quickly -- in just minutes.  With that said, the weakest link in the Top 80 is the ignition which must be checked the moment a pilot senses a degradation in performance.  Changing the spark plug is the cheapest and easiest way to improve performance (assuming it is worn out).  The carburetor must be rebuilt and tested correctly.  Stumbling due to fuel starvation will burn up an engine quickly and why the fuel test must be done if problems are suspected.  The rest pop-off pressure must be within specifications for good idle and low-end performance.  High pop-off pressure will cause the engine to run lean and possibly burn it up.

D. The reed valves should be tested and, if necessary, replaced if you find performance down a bit after a carb rebuild, a spark plug change, and find the ignition system all in order.  That is, the big maintenance items are the usual but if there are still issues, then we look at compression and things like the reed valves.

I regularly do a compression test on my engines and record and compare the values to be sure the top end is in good shape.  If it's 150 psi plus/minus 10% (at sea level) then it's a waste to replace the piston and cylinder. The ignition and carburetor, in that order, are always the first things which fail on the Top 80.  They must be regularly checked.

Reed valve block – A leak here will cause the fuel pump to fail after the engine warms up.  Use blue threadlock on these screws.  Once you check that the block is correctly torqued down, you will not have to check it again.  Technicians at the Miniplane factory routinely do not properly torque down fasteners on new engines which is why pilots must check these things themselves.

This maintenance schedule here is for average use.  If you run at full power most of the time, fly near the ocean, or launch from sandy areas, you may to check things sooner and more often.

After the first 10 hours
     Change the oil in the redrive
     Give the paramotor a careful lookover for loose fittings, especially the motor mount screws (both sides)
     Check the torque of the cylinder head nuts, especially if you run your engine at or near sea level.

Every 25 hours
     Spark plug – replace
     Muffler mounts – regularly check these to be sure they are not broken or loose.  The studs going into the crankcase
          can loosen after many hours and chew up the aluminum threads.  Use high strength (red) threadlock on these studs.

Every year (minimum) – Rebuild carburetor, including replacing the pop-off spring.  Replace fuel filter, change the oil in the redrive,
     replace the spark plug, and check the ignition.  Replace fuel system hoses if ethanol gasoline is used.  If the hoses are stiff, replace them.
     Check the torque of the cylinder head nuts, especially if you run your engine at or near sea level.

Every 50 hours
     Exhaust flange gasket – Check for cracks and excessive wear.  If there are lots of black oil drips in the area, there is also an air leak
          and it should be fixed.  The flange gasket/cylinder head joint should be sealed with high temp sealant e.g. Ultra Grey, as needed.
          This gasket gradually wears out and will crack and leak if left on too long or if the exhaust flange springs are compressed too tightly.
     Clutch – Examine for wear.
     Cylinder head and piston de-carbonizing – Be sure that the piston ring is free and the groove is clean.  A stuck ring will
          greatly affect performance and also tear up the cylinder.  Stuck rings are caused by engine overheating and/or cheap oil.
          This is the most important routine maintenance required on 2 stroke engines, regardless of the fuel type used.
          Always replace the O-ring gasket if the head is removed.
          Starter – Remove and lubricate center bushing with white lithium grease.  DO NOT OVER LUBRICATE!
     Redrive oil – drain & replace.  Be sure to clean the magnetic tip of the drain plug and check the pressure relief valve.

Every 100 hours
     Clutch links, springs, and the inner hub should be checked for excessive wear.  If the links can be wiggled around, the clutch must be
          removed and carefully examined for worn parts.  Go to the clutch page for more details.  Reassemble with anti-seize compound.
     Compression check – this is the #1 way to know what condition the cylinder, piston, and rings are.  If it is out of specs (above), replace
          everything or pilots can also have their cylinders re-bored at this shop for a modest price and get an oversized piston from Miniplane-USA.
     Ignition secondary continuity check -- you can expect the secondary wire to fail by this time.
     Re-drive studs in the engine case  -- make sure they are not loose.  These are SS and will fail after many hours and go into the prop!
     Piston roller-cage bearing replacement, if needed.  Generally, if there is any knocking in the engine, this bearing must be checked/replaced.
     Piston ring replacement, if needed.  If compression is down by over 10%, it is better to replace the piston, ring, and cylinder.
     Prop bolts – check for tightness.  Too tight is as bad as too loose.

Every 150 hours
     Hone cylinder (suggested by Miniplane) –  Pilots can also have their cylinders re-bored/honed at this shop for a modest price and get an oversized
          piston from Miniplane-USA, if needed.

Every 200 hours and more
     Clutch – if the clutch engages at idle or is noisy, the only practical solution is to replace it.
     Cooling ductwork on engine – the aluminum fins that are a part of the cooling box will eventually break loose.  They are not critical parts.
     Cooling fan check – Miniplane recommends replacing it but I am not sure why this needs to be done.
     Crankcase oil seals – replace if leaking beyond an acceptable amount.
     Frame – check it carefully for cracks.  We have a competent shop here who can repair them.  Most welding/muffler shops will ruin your frame.
     Piston – replace if needed.  If there is a serious lack of power because of wear, the cylinder will also need to be replaced/refurbished.
     Reed valve petals – replace (the engine will idle and start better).
