Replacing the propeller shaft seal without disassembly

by Had Robinson

Introduction

As with automotive transmissions, replacing external seals without having to tear the entire piece of equipment apart is always preferable.  The Miniplane prop seal is no different.  Here is how to do it without having to spend hours taking apart and then reassembling the redrive.  This technique is particularly important because the screws holding the redrive together can be difficult to remove â€“ even with heat.  Most amateur mechanics will break them off or strip the top of the screw trying to remove them.

Be SURE to read these instructions all the way through BEFORE beginning this project.

Supplies

Quality electric drill (a drill press is much easier to use), 1/16" drill bit (or the metric equivalent), (1-2) #4 x 3/4" sheet metal screws, 7/8" deep socket, blue threadlock, misc. metric Allen wrenches, gear puller, masking tape, Vaseline, brake cleaner or mineral spirits, Mobil One 75W-90 fully synthetic gear oil or 85W-140 gear oil.

Removing the seal

1. Remove the prop and flange from the shaft.  You will need a gear puller to get it off.  If the flange will not move, maintain the pressure of the puller and then put a torch on it for about 45 seconds.  The aluminum flange will expand and loosen the joint.  When this happens you will usually hear a "ping" sound which is your cue that the hub has loosened.  WD-40 will also help stubborn flanges.  Drain the lubricant from the redrive.  It is best to remove the redrive from the engine.

2. Carefully wrap the shaft with multiple layers of masking tape.  This is CRITICAL.  If you scratch the shaft with the drill or a tool, the new seal will leak and you will likely have to replace the shaft.  Make certain that the tape reaches down to the top of the old seal itself.

 

3. Take the 1/16" bit and SLOWLY (60 RPM max) drill a shallow hole in the side of the old seal.  You must use a quality electric drill as cheap ones will not turn slowly enough.  If you nick the casing, it's not the end of the world as the new seal will have sealant enough to fix any nicks on the casing.  STOP the drill moment the drill starts to dig into the top of the seal.  That is, the moment the drill penetrates the metal just beneath the  rubber coating, it will act like a bolt in a nut and quickly go in.  You must know how to detect this.  We are not trying to make a round hole in the casing of the seal but just penetrate it so we can put a screw in the hole.  If you do it right, you will make an opening in the metal but not go through the rubber on the other side.

If you are unsure of how to do this, practice on a tin can or an old seal.  This is the hardest thing to do and you don't want to make a mistake here.  If you go too far, you will hit the ball bearing that is underneath the seal and scar it.  Thankfully, this bearing has a thin metal retainer protecting the balls and the race but it is still possible to get a drill bit between the cracks and ruin the bearing.  Take care not to contaminate the gear housing with metal chips.  The seal in the housing below was jammed in hard.

 

4. Take a #4 sheet metal screw and carefully screw it about 1 1/2 turns into the hole you made.  YOU MUST USE A #4 SCREW AS A LARGER SIZE WILL NOT WORK.  All the screw has to do is hold against a steady and gentle pull.  If you are careful and the seal is not jammed in too hard, you may only need one screw to pull out the seal.  Use two screws if you cannot get it out with one screw.

 

5. Next, remove any steel flakes with a magnet.  You do not want to contaminate the gear case with steel chips!

 

6. You can use a small block of wood and a pair of wire cutters to grab the screw and GENTLY pull up the seal.  Alternately, a pair of quality Vise-Grips can be used to grab the screw and a screwdriver used to push against the end of the Vise-Grips, as below

 

If you do it right, the seal will pop right out.  If you can, do the last pry with the shaft horizontal or facing a little down, you will eliminate the possibility of junk falling back into the gear case.  If the seal is very tight and cannot be moved with one screw, drill a second hole and try removing it from that side.  You may have to pry a very little bit at a time on each side to work it out.

7. Remove the masking tape from the shaft.  Note the old seal spring floating on the prop shaft.  This was caused when the seal was originally installed by Miniplane too deeply into the shaft housing, causing the spring to separate from the seal.

 

8. After removing the old seal, clean out the old sealant (if there is any) on the casing surface with brake cleaner or equivalent (mineral spirits will work but it is not as good).  To do this, stuff tissue paper down into the shaft housing and then use a toothpick to scrape off the old sealant.  It will fall onto the tissue and then you can make the shaft horizontal and pull out the tissue with the old sealant.  If there is a speck or two of sealant in the redrive, it won't matter.

If you think you contaminated the bearing or the redrive with debris, turn the engine so the shaft is pointing down, put a few ounces of the proper gear oil in the redrive with a plastic pipe or syringe.  Use compressed air on the fill hole to blow the oil out through the bearing.  Turn the shaft a few turns while doing this.  It makes a mess below the shaft but it will thoroughly clean any debris out of the bearing.

9. Use a cotton swap soaked with mineral spirits or brake cleaner (best) to thoroughly clean the seal housing. Use a dry swab to remove any solvent.

10.  Put a layer of masking tape around the shaft.  If you do not do this, the splines on the shaft may cut the seal when it is pushed over them and cause a leak.  The photo below shows the top of the shaft exposed but it, too, should be wrapped in masking tape.

11. Coat the inside of the new seal and shaft with Vaseline.  This will keep the seal from burning up before oil reaches it.  Be careful not to get any Vaseline on the outside rim of the seal or on the gear housing.

 

12. Using a 7/8" deep socket (or the metric equivalent), gently tap the seal down into the housing with a rubber mallet.  Note:  Be EXTREMELY careful not to drive the seal in too far â€“ it is easy to do.  If you have a drill press, use it to press the seal in rather than driving it in with a mallet.
Note:  Tap gently!  The seal will tend to go in crooked.  You must move the socket to the high side and tap/press it down.  Take your time in doing this as you do NOT want to drive the seal in past the housing surface.  A mistake here may require you to use a new seal and start over.

 

 

If you did it right, the new seal will be set even with the bottom of the beveled edge of the housing.

13.  After you drive the seal in, thoroughly de-grease the shaft and propeller flange.  Reassemble the flange to the shaft.  Be sure to use blue thread locker on the center bolt and torque it properly.

14.  Follow these instructions for re-filling the redrive with the correct type and quantity of oil.
