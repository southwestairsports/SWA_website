New paramotor assembly guide

by Had Robinson

Purchased a paramotor?  Pilots need to be careful that it is ready to operate.  Failure to follow the proper steps can ruin an engine.  This checklist is primarily for the Miniplane paramotors but the information is general and can be applied to other paramotors.  If you have any questions about how to prepare and run your new engine, check with your dealer.

WARNING: PARAMOTORS ARE TWO-STROKE ENGINES AND MUST HAVE OIL MIXED WITH THE FUEL!  YOU WILL QUICKLY DESTROY YOUR ENGINE IF YOU DO NOT MIX THE CORRECT AMOUNT OF OIL WITH THE GASOLINE!

A. Oil - fuel mix

All Miniplane paramotors are two-stroke engines and MUST have oil mixed with the fuel.  Here are the mix ratios for the various Miniplane engines.

B. CHT

All pilots should install a CHT (cylinder head temperature gauge) and tachometer/hour-meter on their engines.  The Top 80 with the ABM frame requires the TTO sensor extension #V300-24.  Miniplane-USA has these items.  A CHT will alert pilots to pending problems with their engines, especially the number #1 killer of all paramotors: overheating.  The number #2 killer is failure to mix 2-cycle oil with the fuel, use the correct ratio, or use the correct type of oil.  A CHT will not let you know if the fuel has no oil mixed with it -- the damage from overheating happens too quickly.  Increasing the oil to fuel ratio does nothing to help extend engine life and may ruin them, as noted by all engine manufacturers.  Avoid ethanol fuels.

C. Threadlock

Miniplane may not apply threadlock to some critical fasteners. The following is for the Top 80 but generally applies to all other Miniplane engines.  Make sure that the following have blue threadlock applied:

  • Engine socket button-head frame mounting bolts
  • Exhaust flange studs (use red high strength threadlock instead of blue)
  • Muffler socket button-head bolts
  • Muffler rubber mount studs
  • Screw-on terminal on top of spark plug (use red high strength threadlock instead of blue). Newer sparkplugs may already have this done.
  • Reed valve block mounting screws x 2 (Top 80 only) -- A reed valve block leak will cause the fuel pump to fail.  The carburetor will have to be removed to do this.
  • Check the specifications page of the listed engines for important information, including torque values.  It pays to check the cylinder head nuts for the proper torque (9 Nm) on new or rebuilt Top 80 engines.  Other engines do not seem to have this problem.

D. Assembly

1. Assemble the paramotor according to any instructions received.  If there are not any, this assembly video for a Miniplane Polini will help.  Be sure to install the propeller in the correct position.  I recommend doing a compression check on your new engine and record it in a log.  That way, you can periodically evaluate the actual condition of the important mechanical parts of your engine.

2. If there is a redrive on the motor, be certain that it is filled with the correct type and quantity of oil.  If this is not done, the seals on the redrive will be quickly ruined and the gears and bearings will also quickly wear out.  Be sure to change the oil after the first (10) hours of use.

3. There are (2) areas where serious wear occurs during operation.  Unfortunately, Miniplane does not provide any protection to these surfaces.

a. Be sure to wrap black electrical tape around the throttle cable wherever it contacts the engine frame.  A piece of split hose can also be used.  If you do not do this, the cable will gradually wear away and a.) jam/break the inner throttle cable and/or b.) short the kill switch wire.
b. If you have the Miniplane ABM frame, the side bars will constantly rub and grind away at the (2) upper side sticks where they screw into the frame.  Pilots can wrap the side sticks at the joint with electrical tape or, better, use a piece of automotive heater hose to protect the surfaces.

4. The side sticks will loosen and come out of their sockets after a dozen or so hours.  Take a piece of electrical tape and wrap it around the joint.  DO NOT USE THREADLOCK ON THE SIDE STICKS!

5. Study carefully the section on fuel specifications.  Fuel should be mixed with oil in small batches only (< 2 gallons at a time).  Stored fuel/oil mixes deteriorate rapidly and the lubricating qualities of the oil deteriorate.  Remember do NOT store fuel mixed with oil for more than a few weeks, no matter what kind of gasoline is used.

6. Mix the correct amount of oil with gasoline.  Do exactly what the manufacturer of the engine recommends.  The best mixing bottle is from Shoreline Marine and is sold by Sports Academy and others.  It is simple, well marked, and can be sealed to keep dirt out.  Getting fuel into a Miniplane is difficult without a fuel siphon like this one.  The Chinese knock-offs available at Harbor Freight do not have the right kind of hoses and will kink and collapse once the siphon is started.  The better siphons have a much better quality hose which will last years.  Replacement vinyl tubing for fuel siphons can be purchased from most hardware stores.

7. Prime the carburetor  If this technique is used, the engine will start the first time, every time.  If the carburetor does not have a priming lever, use the end of cotton swab that has had the cotton removed.  Sticking a metal object into the metering lever diaphragm could injure it.  A properly primed engine will rarely need choke except when pilots start their engines while flying and cannot prime them.

8. Avoid running the engine on the ground unless it is grass or clean concrete.  The WORST environment is a beach.  SAND RUINS PARAMOTORS!  Sand gets sucked into the engine and chews up propellers.  Smart pilots will launch from grassy or concrete areas.  If you have to launch from the beach, use only enough throttle to get airborne.

9. After the engine starts, let it idle for a few minutes.  The carburetor must be adjusted for your particular altitude and environment (humid or dry).

10. New engines should be broken-in properly and then only flown for short periods, avoiding full throttle until a full tank of fuel has been consumed.

11. Test fly the engine.  If there are any problems, read this section on performance issues.

It is unlikely that there are ignition or mechanical problems.  The fuel system test is simple.  If the pilot does not want to fix the problem, the results of the test will help the dealer or repair shop.  If the fuel system does not pass the quick test, contact the dealer.  The engine should be returned for a professional evaluation.

Rarely, Miniplane will have an engine that was assembled incorrectly, even damaged and/or missing sealant in critical places.  A professional can find the problem and fix it quickly while an amateur may ruin the engine.

12.  If the engine will be stored for more than a few weeks, purge the fuel system.  If this is not done, the engine may not start again until the carburetor is rebuilt.  In any case, carburetors should be rebuilt yearly.  Ethanol fuels attack and destroy fuel system parts as well as mix poorly with 2 cycle oils.
