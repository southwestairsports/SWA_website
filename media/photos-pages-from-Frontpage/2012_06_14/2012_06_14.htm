06/06/16

 

June 14 Thursday – The Days are Just Packed – Being that this time of year is synonymous with goofy air, we take what we can get.  Then there are times when we get more than we think - or deserve.  Make no mistake, the thermals during late spring, summer, and early fall are ballistic for us paragliders during most of the day.  But, occasionally we have exceptions to the rules, especially freedom from high winds aloft – today was one of them as late spring tends to be really turbulent around here.  Being that our other PG gung-ho local pilot, LB, had to w*rk, I (Had Robinson) was on my own.  Starting just before 7PM, there was good steady air from the WSW at 12 mph which makes the Franklin Mountains the real "Magic Kingdom."

Below, the increasing moisture creeping in from the southeast is evident.  Though we do not have the vistas as usual, it is a pleasant change.  The pilot here is about 1/2 mile west of the summit of N. Mt. Franklin looking north.  The Organ Mountains are barely visible in the distance.  Smooth and steady lift was everywhere!

Southwest of the N. Mt. Franklin summit, the pilot must work hard to bench up out of the slower air.  Once above 6,500' MSL or so, the winds aloft are the same day or night.  Below this elevation, they quickly die down at the end of the day due to the creation of the daily inversion which isolates this lower air from that higher.  However, this pilot did not work too hard getting back up because he had a paramotor which could be started and provided what the diminishing winds could not:  Lift!

Flying to the Triangle, he encountered the amazing lift which, without any sort of external power, always gets us up to the top of the range.  The photo below is just south of the summit of N. Mt. Franklin.  The air about 1/4 mile to the right (or west) is the "sweet spot" of the range.  Here thermals and lift combine into a single stream that goes – up!  It is a band of lift that is wide and very easy to stay in.

A view out in front of N. Mt. Franklin looking east.  The white speck is an Amateur Radio Repeater hosted by the West Texas Repeater Association.  The frequency (input) is 146.88 MHz. Solar powered, it has an amazing range - about 70 miles in every direction.

The photo below is the same as the one two above but taken just before dusk.  It is a striking view being in the mountains at this time of day.  It is hard to describe what it is like – gliding along in silky smooth air with only the rush of air.  There were vultures and swifts soaring above the peak and their cries were easy to hear.  But it was time to land and it takes a while to get down and you have to plan on it so you do not have to land in the dark.

Hope you enjoyed the show!
