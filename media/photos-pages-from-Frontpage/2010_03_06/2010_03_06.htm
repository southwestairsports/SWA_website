 Dry Canyon - rock work & flights - Saturday, March 6, 2010

RGSA President Robin Hastings (HG) led a pack of pilots up the hill to launch.  They included:  Lee Boone (PG), Mike Ellsworth (HG), visiting pilot Jason Cade, Had Robinson (PG), and driver Bahrat Kumar - a PhD student and friend of Robin's.  We are expecting some photos of Jason and Lee soon - we will update this page.

Conditions were weak - weaker than most had ever seen at this site.  At times, winds at launch were calm.  Rather than bemoan the poor conditions to fly, we all spent an hour or two gathering huge rocks for the upcoming fix-up day where we will do some major repairs on the Dry Canyon launch pad used primarily for HG pilots.

Conditions became better around 2PM with a 6-8 mph breeze from the SW.  Lee Boone and Jason Cade launched and got right above launch but were unable to stay in the lift band as conditions weakened again.  It's about a ten minute cycle and pilots must be exceedingly careful during the lulls not to sink out -- staying up at those times is part of the learning curve in mastering Dry Canyon.

Aborted Launch  Had Robinson prepared to launch after Lee and Jason but had to abort.  He noticed that his reserve handle was hanging loose during the routine pre-flight check.  Another pilot was being helpful and accidently grabbed the handle and yanked it, releasing the pins which hold the reserve in place in the harness.  Flying with the reserve loose can result in an accidental deployment - it's about 50-50 that it will come out, especially during turbulence in flight or setting up for landing.  Had had to stop the launch process and reset the reserve in its container on the harness.  He then launched without incident and flew at the face near launch until it started raining, his first time to ever experience it in the air.  Rain is not good for PG as too much can be absorbed by the wing and drastically change its flying characteristics.  Rather than guess if it would end soon (which it did), he jammed the speed bar to "full" and made a bee-line to the LZ, landing safely after a 40 minute flight.  If it starts to rain hard, pilots must make an emergency landing immediately.

We welcome pilot PG Jason Cade who recently moved from Salt Lake City to undertake engineering studies at UT El Paso.

Here are some photos of our day yesterday - more will be posted as they come in.  We had quite a crowd of visitors at launch Saturday afternoon including a carload of youth, two off-roader's, and two ATV's.  Robin and his glider can just be seen at launch.

Launch from directly above - Robin Hastings is preparing to launch.  The man in front of Robin's glider with the blue jacket is Mike Ellsworth, a hang glider pilot assisting Robin.  The cycles were weak but, because of an approaching storm, they began to grow in strength.  Robin is waiting for just the right moment.

Robin, our weather expert, knew that conditions for hang gliding were too weak so he makes for the LZ.  Robin has never had any serious mishaps after decades of hang gliding.  Why is this?  It is not luck but is his determination to minimize or eliminate risk.  Launching and flying in bad conditions (above our skill level), doing maneuvers near the ground, and not being ready to land at any time probably eliminates 90% of our risk.  Robin is an expert in these three aspects and many more....

We had about 15 visitors at launch who watched the soaring pilots at Dry Canyon.
