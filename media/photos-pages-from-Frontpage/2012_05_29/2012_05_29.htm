06/06/16

 

May 29 Tuesday – Northeastern Oklahoma – Powered paragliding has few of the exciting challenges or the peacefulness of cross-country free flight but it sure has one thing: CONVENIENCE. If the photo below was from a cross country flight, the pilot would be setting up to land! Under power, we can go for mile after mile a few hundred feet above the terrain, taking it all in in close-up fashion. So, I took another trip and went to the dam that created Lake Tenkiller. Rather than fly along the lake shore, I flew south from Tahlequah, OK over a dirt road that went for over 20 miles through the bucolic countryside on my way to the Tenkiller dam. Living in El Paso, it is easy to forget that some areas of the country are "green", a strange color but prevalent in other parts of the world. Below is "country roads" revisited in the peaceful backcountry of NE Oklahoma.

Mile after mile I flew straight south over this dirt road which, at various times, petered out and popped up again.  On either side were various ranches and farms.  To the left in the far distance is the former Illinois River valley which is now Lake Tenkiller.

This one property had a hidden nook where an inviting swimming hole with a diving board could be found.  I suppose if I had landed there, the owners might have permitted a dip.  The people in the region are friendly and helpful.

As I went south, the Lake turned more to the southwest and became visible.  Why didn't I fly down the Lake?  Trees go right down to the shoreline!  If a pilot has an engine out, where would he land?  Drowning is the #1 cause of death for PPG pilots so I wished to avoid the possibility.

A beautiful country estate about 4 miles west of the lake near its south end.  There are no trains, no freeways, and the area is removed from any common flight paths.  This means one thing:  Quiet (my flying over didn't help but it certainly was a novelty, in any case.)

I wanted to see the dam.  In order to safely cross the Lake (where landing on the shore would not be safe or easy), a pilot would have to climb up to an altitude high enough to glide to a safe LZ – which I did here.  The dam is just to the right of the photo.

Lake Tenkiller dam – few realize that there is a small hydroelectric plant at the dam (the brown roofed structure in front of a white silo-like tower in the photo below).  Those who look closely will note that the there is no overflow at the dam.  Conditions all across the southwest and central plains have been very dry.  This part of Oklahoma has had but 20% of the normal rainfall for this time of year.

The "wash" from the generators is clearly visible in the photo below.  The lake is about 10' lower than normal.

After the 1 1/2 hour flight to the dam, I decided to land and relax at in open field about 3 miles northeast of the dam.  The Lake is about 100 yd. beyond the tree line in the distance.  A curious onlooker could not believe it when I dropped out of the sky.

After relaxing for about an hour, I re-launched in the nearly dead air and high-tailed it back to Tahlequah.  The sky west of this area was starting to darken (visible in the photo above to the left).  Soon, the weather would overdevelop and it would not be smart to be caught in any possible outflow from towering clouds dumping a million tons of cold air to the ground.  Flying the Paramania Revo2 (reflex) glider is a joy as a pilot can let the trimmers all the way out and still go on full speed bar with great confidence.  Top speed going back (about 20 miles as the crow flies) was over 40 m.p.h.

Learning first how to paraglide without a motor is a good plan for safety.  As an instructor, I am amazed at the number of avoidable accidents PPG pilots experience because they choose to do any sort of maneuver near the ground (less than 500' AGL).  For example, when I returned to Tahlequah on full speed bar with the trimmers completely out, I had over 2000' of altitude above the ground – just in case.  While 98% of the time we would have nothing to worry about, we need to be at 100%.  Why be among the 2% that experience problems and then get drilled into the ground, as someone recently did at the Beach Blast in Florida?

Experienced pilots and instructors know what can happen and that is why we take consistent measures to avoid risk.

I hope you enjoyed the show!
