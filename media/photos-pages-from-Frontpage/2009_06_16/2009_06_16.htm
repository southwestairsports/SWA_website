 June 16, 2009 Soaring at Kilbourne Hole Maar

Pilots Had Robinson and Lee Boone ventured out Tuesday to fly the maar in the late afternoon.  All of the forecasts said that the air would be around 8 from the SW, changing to the WNW around 6 PM.

We got there and it was averaging over 18 -- too much to fly in.  The air mellowed and Had launched from the east rim (visible in the photo below) and immediately got strong lift -- as high and as far out in front as anyone has ever been (200'+ over launch).  For five minutes it was strong air and then it began to get turbulent. 

As good pilots, when the air gets horsey, you get high for safety.  Sure enough, the area south of launch which was huge lift now became huge sink so it was good to be out front and high so you could choose your LZ.

The sudden presence of sink is what happens when the air switches from SW to NW.  Because the launch site is a point that sticks out into the maar, it is easy to launch in winds as much as 160 degrees difference.  The downside is that if you are flying low on the upwind side of launch and there is a wind shift, you can get caught in air that is going down.  The safety aspect is that all wind quickly dies down the lower you go in the maar.  This is generally not the case anywhere else.  So, air that is going down rapidly becomes air that isn't doing anything = a safe landing.

The pilot found a spot where the mesquite was more distant apart and landed.  However, the air still was shifting a little and his glider wound up in some bushes (not mesquite!). 

Kilbourne Hole Maar sink out -- Here is what happens when you fly in air that's changing directions -- you sink out!  A few minutes ago, the pilot was happily flying 500' above the ground and then -- wham -- the wind changed and this area south of launch (the rim far in the distance) became air that was going down!  It was a nice hike back to the top.  The wind became steady an hour later and soaring the Maar became perfect!

Had the pilot had just 4' more, he would have missed the creosote bush.  But all was well -- landing in the bushes is part of living in some of the most amazing flying country in the world.  We pretty much have this region to ourselves -- it's pretty remote and off the grid.  Cell phones do work at launch because of the altitude but nowhere else.

Below is an image of the first flight.  It's easy to see how horsey the air was and how there was super lift one minute and then super sink the next.  We would not fly in these conditions (expecting a wind direction/weather change in the mountains but here it is a different story because of the protection from wind below the lip of the maar.  Check the next image for how flying is when the air settles down.  It is quite a difference.

Below is why soaring a maar is so much fun!  It is one of the few inland sites in the US where pilots can consistently easily top-land without the danger of rotor or turbulence.  The face of the maar is gentle as well as the back.  The pilot here flew until it was almost 8:30PM.  Who knows what we might do on a moonlit night???
