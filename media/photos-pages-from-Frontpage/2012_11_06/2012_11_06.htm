November 6 Tuesday – The Chihuahuan Desert –   There is one word that describes this desert located in the Horse latitudes of the northern hemisphere: VAST.  Flying over the region is like flying over the ocean.  The pilot here took a trip west from the Gardiner sod farms to the west side of the East Potrillo Mountains, north to Kilbourne Hole, and then southeast back to the sod farms – a 50 mile trip.

Heading west – the East and West Potrillo Mountains are visible in the far distance.  Mt. Riley is the peak in the middle.

Flying PPG has its advantages over PG in that the pilot can fly for long periods very close to the ground – even touching the tips of the cactus with his feet.  Here is one of the occasional oases in this desert.  The water for the cattle (and other critters) in this particular tank is trucked in from many miles away.

Another view of the Chihuahuan Desert, the largest in the western hemisphere, looking south deep into Mexico.  This is dark sky country where only satellite phones and Amateur Radio work.  Even the occasional communications tower must be powered solar cells – there are no utilities and water is about 600' below the surface.

This is the west face of the East Potrillo Mountains (Mt. Cox and the West Potrillo Mountains are in the distance).  The view here is northwest.  I was able to ridge soar the ridge to the right in the photo below.  Winds were south at 6 and hit the range at an angle but it was still soarable – barely.

Another advantage of PPG is that a pilot can safely go over the back of mountains from the windward side.  In PG, this can be a terrifying experience because a pilot cannot be sure that he can fly high enough to avoid the rotor behind the ridge.  In the photo below I am downwind of the range.  I climbed out on the windward site and then darted downwind over the top of the range.  Just like at a dam in a river, the air at the top edge of the range was moving fast – over 30 mph.  It was fun getting whisked over the top and then catapulted downwind.

This is another "hueco" or tank.  It was made in a natural low spot by some rancher decades ago and then carefully lined with clay so that it would not leak.  The water in it is from past rainfall and can remain for many months – until the next rain.  These tanks rarely run dry and provide water for all thirsty creatures in the desert.

Hunt's Hole is a few miles south of Kilbourne Hole which is visible in the distance.  Both maars never fill with water because of the huge lava dooms that are deep beneath them in the earth.  If a hiker visits the bottom of these maars he will see small drain holes (6" in diameter or so) and huge cracks where the water and mud go after a rain.  I have seen drain holes at the bottom of Hunt's that are almost big enough to crawl down....  One can only imagine the size of the dome below that never fills up.  The view here is north.  The maar is about 1 mile in diameter.

Kilbourne Hole – the east ridge of the maar is almost 400' above the bottom of the maar which makes it a fine ridge soaring site when winds are westerly – one of the safest in the region.

This is the view mile after mile after mile back to the sod farms. (The Franklin Mountains are in the distance.)  Going along 20' over the ground is great sport.  Desert travelers that leave tracks can be comforted to know that the wind storms we have every few months will obliterate the evidence completely.  The mounds are formed by the blowing sand getting caught by the mesquite trees and can be up to 15' high.  If it were not for these tough trees (and enough rainfall to keep them alive), the landscape would be quite different, more like here.

Hope you enjoyed the show!
