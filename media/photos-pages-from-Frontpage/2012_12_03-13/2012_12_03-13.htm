VALLE DE BRAVO 2012

December 3 - 8 – Thermalling is the skill everyone learns here as there are plenty of strong thermals that can take pilots over a mile into the air.  An important extra is the Mexican ambience rarely found by ordinary tourists – the friendly people and the geography of the mountainous central areas of Mexico.

Below, the famous El Peñon.  This towering rocky peak is a great source of thermals for pilots.  The view is north from a fertile mesa that is at it's foot.

A crowded day above the main launch with over (30) paragliders and hang gliders in the air.  View is north east.  El Peñon is to the left of the photo about a mile.

The El Torre launch just east of the town of Valle de Bravo.  This is a difficult launch because of the trees on either side and the steep ramp.

However, La Torre is perfect for lawn darts – as seen below.  Kip from Maine is the pilot.

After boating around mountains behind Valle for a few hours, pilots have to land at the little square here.  Landing skills are important!  A red paraglider can just be seen at the tip of the arrow.  In wet years, this postage stamp of an LZ is all there is.  Everything else is under water.  It is not hard to set up – and why a visit with the local pilots first is a good idea.

If a pilot gets really high, he can fly out over the lake, as here, and enjoy some stunning views of this little town in the mountains.  The LZ is just to the right of the VB Yacht Club.

Our group having lunch at Jovan's.  We are with Fly Mexico.  L-R: Jo & Jack, Damien, John, Jeff Hunt (our host), and Evelyn & Andrew

The main LZ late in the afternoon.  Peñon is to the left and the main launch is just visible in the upper right.

The top of El Peñon – a glider is just visible going up near cloud base.

When a pilot lands, he has plenty of time – waiting to get picked up.  There are no animals around except cows, horses, dogs, and a few birds.  There are very few flies and virtually no mosquitoes.  The land is volcanic in origin and incredibly rich.  Native corn grows thick and is not attacked by bugs or deer (there are none here).  But there are occasional things of beauty in the bug department, like this garden spider...

An LZ known as Las Peñitas.  Note the grass – thick and green and a great place to nap.

Some pilot high above flying his heart out.

A neighbor of the spider above.  These creatures are the ones that spin amazing and beautiful webs.  Some of their stringers can stretch a span of (10) yards.  I walked into one of these webs and it was not 5 minutes until the spider got back to work (America take note...).

The common Morning Glory – with a honey bee and some other small bugs.  Flowers depend on the bugs to spread genetic material around.

Kip from Maine getting his hang glider ready for launch at Peñon.

Kip ready to go!

View of El Peñon from above launch.  Behind it is "The Wall" – a place of more crazy thermals.

It's late in the day – the mist is thick and the air boaty.  It is such a contrast from the air of our home in the southwest U.S. The shiny things in the lower right are ponds reflecting the setting sun.  This place has water and soil like few other places.

It's time to land – it's important to be able to see the LZ.

This modest road sign marks the restaurant that every pilot from all over the world loves.  The lady who runs it did something that works in every language:  Bring the customers into the kitchen and let them pick out their dish.  No surprises!  With fresh tortillas made on the location and locally grown vegetables, it is the place of choice.  Then there is the price – under $5 for a huge multi-course meal.  Of course, the food is delicious.

Organically grown corn.  The soil is so rich the farmers don't have to till the soil – the weeds grow alongside the corn.  When you have tortillas, you don't need bread.

If you get REALLY high over La Torre launch, you can fly out over the lake with plenty of altitude to spare, as I did here.  I beat my own record by finding some tractor-beam from outer space to get to 8,000' MSL over this soaring site in the late afternoon.  Want to retire here?  For about $600/month you get a villa on the water with a cook, maid, and a gardener.  I am tempted....

My hotel room looking out.  Fresh flowers are put everywhere.  The Mexicans have art in their blood.  They don't build anything without making it a thing of beauty – even the town streets are cobble and hand-shaped.

An inviting garden within the walls of the little hotel where I am staying.

Last but not least, the Norwegians (30 of them) visited Valle this year to fly.  Here they are lounging around in the main LZ below the Peñon launch.  They have the most women pilots.  The Germans had zero and we had two.  The woman in the pink shirt is a beginner and had a huge collapse flying out in front of launch – she has kept flying and was not scared out of the sport.  Her bravery is commendable.  Every week, we have several reserve parachute tosses by pilots flying in air way above their skill level on twitchy gliders.  So far, no one has been hurt and everyone has had way too much fun.  More later....
