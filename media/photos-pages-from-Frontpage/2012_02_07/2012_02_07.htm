06/06/16

 

February 7 Tuesday – E. Potrillo Mountains – a record setting flight!  Early in the day, it looked like we would be blown out.  The forecast had gusting with very high winds.  But this is why we watch the real-time air at the Santa Teresa Airport:  Things started to calm down in the early afternoon.  Everything was lining up for strong air (+15) right out of the east which is perfect for the E. Potrillo Mountains.  But what about the gusting?  The forecast was base wind + 5 which is not excessive for paragliding.

So I (Had) threw the switch at around 3PM.  Time to move it - grab all of the gear and jump in the truck as there would not be a whole lot of time to drive the 30+ miles.  I still had to do the trek up to the Torrey Paso launch that is just below a ridge in the range.  When you are alone, your launch has to be perfect.  In high winds, that means setting up the glider on the ground in such a way (a tight but inflatable rosette) that it will not get inflated by the air until the pilot is ready.  When it's time to go, the glider must be brought up perfectly square as the pilot cannot launch crosswise into the wind as that would take him right over the back.  Before the glider even gets overhead, you are in the air and must be ready to touch the speed bar as going over the back of the ridge could be an adventure.

Everything went well and I got up and off!  It was a grand flight, the highest and longest yet in the East Potrillo Mountains.

Over the top of the range looking northwest.  Mt. Riley (R) and Mt. Cox (L) are in the distance.

Looking over the highest peak in the East Potrillo Mountains.  The lift band today was the strongest a pilot could safely fly in.  We should never fly in air where we need speed bar to penetrate forward in the air.  The air at altitude was 20+ mph.  In the very far distance are the volcanic cones of the West Potrillo Mountains.

Near the north end of the range.  I got to the the little peak in the lower right but dared not go any further as it would be too easy to get out of the lift band and have to land somewhere many miles from launch.  Saddles and the ends of ranges are vortices where the air quickly speeds up and it is very easy to get sucked into them.  Pushing your fortunes here would mean 6-7 mile hike through the desert...

Over the top of the range looking south.  It was getting late in the day and, in such strong conditions, it is not wise to land in the dark.  The Torrey Paso LZ is that white area at the base of the range to the left.  Note how far out in front the lift band is.  I am over a mile in front of the range.  I landed halfway from the LZ to launch.

Hope you enjoyed the show!  Learn how to do this safely with us.
