 Dry Canyon Adventure Part II
August 8th 2009

Lee Boone, Had Robinson (pilots) and Marilyn & Tirzah Robinson (drivers) headed off to launch late Saturday and arrived about 5 PM.  Winds were strong (gusting to 25 mph) at launch so Lee & Had hiked around the ridge.  We decided to wait about an hour and a half to be sure that things had calmed down as we had to be certain that there was no chance of getting blown over the back of the ridge after launch.  For a two minute video of the highlights, go here.

We launched just before 8PM and went quickly up in the 12-15 mph air that was coming straight in.  Both pilots had an easy time getting 500' over launch in the laminar air.  If we had crystal balls, we would have launched earlier but, generally, pilots must be absolutely certain of conditions at world-class sites such as Dry Canyon.  Cascading events can get pilots in big trouble so they must do everything possible to minimize risk which means, "If you don't feel 100%, don't fly."

This view is of the Dry Canyon ridge.  Launch is just to the lower right of the photo.  The towering ridge of the Sacramento's (9,500') is visible in the background.  The desert presents a beautiful sunset glow off of the formerly active systems that had developed during the day.

We could not spend much time soaring the ridge because it would mean landing in the dark.  We headed out to the LZ but only at 7-8 mph over the ground because of the high winds aloft.  To ensure that we got to the LZ as quickly as possible, we applied speed bar which increased our over the ground speed to over 12 mph.

Flying in the desert sunset is magic!  The San Andres mountain range of New Mexico is just visible at the bottom of the photo.  (The pilot below is Lee Boone - this is his first time to land at the main LZ for Dry Canyon.)

 

We are just about out of light - it's time to land!  The only turbulence encountered was by Had as he flew behind and below Lee and encountered the vortices that spin off the wing tips of a glider.  This swirling air moves out and down.  The air over Alamogordo was moving up everywhere in the "glass off" which made our trip from launch even longer.  We landed at the main LZ in dead air which meant a significant run.  It was another magic flight at Dry!

"So many mountains - so little time!"
