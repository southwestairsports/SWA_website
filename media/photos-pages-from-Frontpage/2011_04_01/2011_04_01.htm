Friday – April 1 – The Mexican Border

The air was a pussycat today with winds aloft of 5-10 mph or so up to 20K' MSL.  What was interesting and unusual was that that winds were south to 6K' and then suddenly did a 180 degree switch to the north above this altitude.  As I flew through the boundary between the air masses, there was a bit of turbulence and a sudden drop in temperature.  How can this happen?  Air masses of different temperatures do not tend to mix so they stay separated.  The weather balloon soundings from today show it all (columns 7 & 8).  We study these things so we do not have surprises.

Looking due south across the U.S. – Mexican border late in the day when the shadows are long.  The Chihuahuan desert is vast and the air is clear.  The border is noted by the change in desert vegetation (near the upper third of the photo).  The U.S. side is barren and covered with mesquite trees while the Mexican side, though appearing barren, has more grass.  Presumably, the Mexicans got rid of the noxious mesquite long ago or they did not let their lands get overgrazed, as here.  The mountains in the far distance are 140 miles away and are part of the Sierra Madre.  Mexico is a beautiful and wild country.

A view of western Juarez, Mexico looking southeast – the most dangerous city in the world.  The green arrows show the international border.  Don't worry, the U.S. side is jammed with U.S. Border Patrol agents and the fence on the border is 16' high and very difficult to scale so it is safe to fly here.

The is photo is not of the border but a view of the Franklin Mountains looking east.  In the distance is the Tularosa Basin with Sierra Alto way in the distance.  It was fun to fly near the top of the peaks and "peak" over the top to the other side.  I was planning on going much higher but it was time to come down – flying at night is dicey because it's hard to see the ground in order to land....
