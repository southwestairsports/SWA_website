 June 8th - 9th, 2012 The Little Bear Fire - near Ruidoso, NM

Ski Apache has turned on the snow machines to protect the buildings!

These are pictures taken by Had Robinson of the Little Bear fire near Ruidoso, NM.

Friday afternoon at Ski Apache looking northwest.  We had camped there for the purpose of doing some site work at Ski Apache.  It didn't seem like much was going on and neither the great folks at Ski Apache nor the authorities had asked anyone to evacuate.

Below – about 30 minutes later than the photo above.  The effects of the strong winds can be seen.  Still no word from the authorities - so we left our RV, equipment, and dog Max in the parking lot at Ski Apache and went down the mountain for dinner with friends.

Saturday, June 9th.  We were unable to go back up Ski Run Road (Hwy 532) to fetch our dog and equipment so we spent the night with friends in Ruidoso.  It was not a good night for us as we had visions of our equipment destroyed and our dog being burned alive.  The next morning we got up at dawn and got permission to go back up to Ski Apache and fetch everything.  We are extremely grateful to the Fire Command for letting us return to our site.  The photo below was at 6:30AM about 3 miles down from the top of Ski Run Rd (Hwy 532) at its most northerly point.  The fire was increasing in intensity in the North Eagle Creek canyon.

Below – Ski Apache early Saturday morning, June 9th.  Personnel were preparing to spray the buildings with water.  We were able to hook up our RV, get Max, gather up all of our equipment (especially our stored gasoline), and leave.

Below – Ski Run Road a few hundred yards from the top.  We knew it was time to beat it.  Thankfully, the ski area is in the lee of the strong winds from the southwest so they were not anywhere near as strong as west of here and higher.  If winds had been from the west, this area would have been an inferno like what later happened in the Monjeau Peak area.

Below – The dead tree above the mirror was set on fire by the "fingers" of fire creeping along the ground here and there.  If there had been any serious winds, it would have been all over for us and we would have lost everything.

Below – The fire spreading closer to the road.  I was frightened by the prospect of a burning tree falling across the road and blocking our escape.  Thankfully, I had the equipment to quickly move heavy timber in case something like that might have happened.  You do what you have to do.

The winds were not in danger of changing direction. If they had been, we would not have attempted to rescue our equipment and dog.  We ought to know about weather.  It has been my business for many years to carefully study the weather because our safety as ultralight pilots depends on it.  We knew (as well as everybody else in the whole world who cares about such things) that Friday and Saturday were going to be blow-outs.  The fire would go in the direction of the winds – which it did (and always does).

Below – Looking northeast from Ski Run Road just below its most northerly portion – North Eagle Creek and environs.

Below – The deserted Hwy 48 - the fire crossed the road north of here and only emergency traffic was permitted.  We are headed south and away from the fire.

Below – The fire raging above Monjeau Peak (next two photos)

Below – Looking north from our safe spot at the inferno above Monjeau Peak (next two photos).

We hope that the firefighters are able to end this devastation as soon as possible!
