 Dry Canyon Adventure

July 18, 2009

Pilots:  Lee Boone & Had Robinson

Driver(s):  Grady Viramontes & Martha Desmond

Photographers: Grady & Had

Group met at the LZ and was at launch at about 6:30PM.

Below Lee is just barely visible at launch in the red/white Niviuk Koyot.

There was quite a bit of OD (cloud over development) but it was rapidly fading away.  A high level haze also suppressed thermal generation.  This was all good as Lee Boone had never flown Dry before and is a relatively new P2.

Below, Lee launched and completed his turn back.  Grady & Martha are barely visible at the ramp - they were a big help in getting the pilots into the air.  Lee is the first graduate of our school and the first native born and locally trained PG pilot to fly Dry Canyon.

Instructor Had gave Lee a good briefing.  Had launched around 7:30PM and Lee shortly thereafter.  The conditions were perfect for newer pilots - air was coming straight in at launch between 10-14 mph and thermic activity was receding to near zero.  These conditions gave plenty of lift out in front of the ridge.

Below, Lee is flying out in front of the ridge with Alamogordo in the background.  The West Ridge of Dry Canyon xxxis to the right.  The main LZ is to the left of Lee's left wing tip.

Lee practiced ridge soaring rules and where to fly in order to stay in lift.  Had was able to get high over the ridge and take some great videos and photos of Lee from the air.

Below, Sissy, Grady's dog, is watching from the launch ramp.  She wants to fly, too!

Grady and Martha took many photos of this first flight of Dry Canyon by a graduate of our school.  Lee & Had flew the ridge for about an hour and safely landed in the bailout LZ area about one miles south of the Space Museum.  Had landed at the main LZ.

Below, Lee comes around a corner of the ridge towards the ramp.  The lift was excellent out in front.

 

Had (right) and Lee (left) flying out from the face of the ridge towards the LZ.  It's around 8PM and the light is becoming ethereal.  The White Sands are visible in the distance.
