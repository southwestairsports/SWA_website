 Mt. Riley - Thermal & Soaring Paradise

November 11, 2009

Pilots Lee Boone & Had Robinson set off for the hike 'n fly up Mt. Riley to launch in ideal soaring & thermalling conditions.  Careful study of the atmospheric soundings (SkewT) are very helpful for soaring pilots - our thanks to the National Weather Service.

Both pilots were rewarded.  Lee set a record for in air time in the region of 2 hours.  Had set an altitude record for the region of almost 9,000'.  This is the high desert and conditions can be very strong so we are always careful.

Below is an image of Had's flight.  The wavy lines on the right are actually tight circles made by the pilot in order to stay in the thermal.  Notice how the thermal here drifts back in the direction of the wind.  Because it is late fall and the atmosphere was cooling at a modest rate with a gain in altitude, the thermals were not strong.  This is why the thermal paths are not very vertical.  Had flew in multiple thermals, as seen below. 

There was an inversion at 11,500' so top altitude was limited.  It was easily visible at 9,000' -- an eerie sight.  While Had could have gone up another 1,500' or so, pilots flying in this remote region need to stay together for safety.  As he was drifting north over the mountains and out of sight, he left the thermal he was in and headed back south.

Lee Boone stayed in ridge lift in front of Mt. Riley until the sun was gone.  Had improvised an "aircraft beacon" and "runway light" using the old "ground fire" technique.  It served multiple duty by also being a wind direction indicator (a.k.a. smoke drift).  As Lee was landing. the katabatic flow off the mountains really kicked in.  In high mountains, this flow can really complicate landing making it a downwind landing and "hot."  In some parts of Alaska, this flow can exceed 160 mph.  The famous Santa Ana winds of southern California are also katabatic winds.

Had cruised the mountains in the area, exploring the south and west face of Mt. Cox for the first time.  Mt. Riley has fewer deep canyons which improves the lift there.  Cox might be good in strong west winds, nonetheless.  Riley's cone shape still provides the best possibilities for convergence of ridge lift and thermals.

We hiked to the top with full gear - it was a chore!  Lee hiked to the top twice as he sank out the first time and wanted a chance to soar again.  He had success!
