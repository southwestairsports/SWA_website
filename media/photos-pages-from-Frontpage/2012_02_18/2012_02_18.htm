06/06/16

 

February 18 Saturday – Sod Farm & Franklin Mountains Gangbusters – Cybele Christine & Jose Muñoz were our heroes at the sod farm today – our winch was out of commission so it was a kiting day.  But it could not have been better – a gentle breeze coming in from the north around 4-6 mph.  The pilots practiced "variation 3"  – a way to hold the glider in complete control when at launch (or kiting).  Both pilots were able to do forward launches in the gentle air.  Overall, it was a day of glider handling #101.  If a pilot learns how to handle his glider on the ground, he can handle it in the air.

Then it was gangbusters in the Franklin Mountains! Instructor Had Robinson launched from Agave Hill in perfect conditions: The air was coming in around 5-8 mph right out of the west. Agave behaved in perfect form. A pilot must turn north from launch and get a few hundred feet over launch before turning south and getting the "big lift" out in front of the Triangle. Today was not strong aloft so you had to be patient getting up in the Triangle. The thermals have a period of about 5 minutes so you cannot give up. Once at the top of the Franklins, Had decided to do some cross country and went south along the range as far as he could without having to land downtown somewhere. Today with the west winds, he got within about 2 miles of Stanton Street. When a pilot passes Coronado Country Club the range makes a dog-leg more southeast and the crest drops dramatically. It can fool you – a pilot thinks he is still high but he is sinking down and will fight upwind all the way back. Only one pilot has ever made the round trip from Agave all the way to the end of the Franklins and back.

The southern end of the Franklin Mountains.  I got just about as far as the radio tower in the foreground but had to turn back – lift was going away and I would have to land in some obscure location where pickup would be difficult.

Below is an image of the track log of today's flight.  Notice how long it took to get up at the Triangle and how far down the range the pilot went.  This flight was a classic Agave – fly north from launch, get high in the house thermal, turn south, work up at the Triangle – and the sky is yours!  The blip to the left in the Triangle was a safety measure.  The pilot had no idea if the sudden lift experienced there was from higher (and dangerous) winds aloft or a powerful thermal rolling in.  It was a powerful thermal, especially for so late in the day when thermals tend to be weak.  Generally, the thermals were modest (<600'/min).
