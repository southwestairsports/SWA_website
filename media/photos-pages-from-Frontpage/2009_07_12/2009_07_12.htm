06/06/16

 

 July 12, 2009 Dry Canyon

Pilots Robin Hastings (HG) and Had Robinson (PG) took on Dry Canyon on a hot dry afternoon in July.

Below Robin is soaring out in front of Dry Canyon.  The White Sands and the clouds blend together in the background.

Guests were Grady Viramontes and friend Sara (Las Cruces) and first flight babe, Marilyn Robinson (El Paso).  Grady and Marilyn helped drive the vehicles back down the mountain.  Thank you!

Had Robinson sits at the edge of the launch ramp while watching Robin soaring.

 

As is typical, hang gliders can (and prefer to) launch in stronger conditions than paraglider pilots so Robin launched first and Had waited about an hour to launch when conditions weakened slightly.

Robin flying near launch.

While the incoming air at launch rarely went above 12 mph, the thermals were strong and booming.  In fact, both Robin and Had ran into horsey thermals right off launch (7,000') so both pilots decided to fly away from the ridge to find gentler air.  What they did find were gigantic thermals with sustained lift over 1,000'/min.  Had's vertical speed indicator (variometer) recorded brief periods of lift exceeding 3,500'/min.  Whenever soaring pilots fly in summer conditions in the desert, they can expect very strong air from about 10:30AM to 5:30PM.  Outside of midday, the strength of thermals weakens more and more.  Within an hour of sunrise or dusk, there is virtually no more thermals being created.

Had is soaring in front of the launch area.  He is flying a small Ozone UltraLite.

Thankfully, the edges of the thermals were not anything as sharp a mile away from the ridge.  After soaring the ridge, Had flew to the LZ and continued to go up -- to over 8,200'.

Below Grady Viramontes is practicing inflating a paraglider at the Dry Canyon LZ.  The DC launch is just visible at the far left of the photo.

Both pilots landed safely in the gentle SW breeze that was at the LZ.  After both pilots landed, Had worked with Grady on improving his kiting skills -- it's a dance with the glider and the wind.  Even if we don't fly, we are in beautiful places and the fellowship is great!

This is the object of kiting - get the wing up overhead safely and with 100% control.  Note the huge LZ provided by the City of Alamogordo.  What cities in the U.S. would be so generous?  We are very thankful.

Here is a track of Had's flight.  Note the width of the broad and high part of the track in the middle.  This thermal was enormous -- over a mile and a half wide.  It was located out in front of the north wall of Dry Canyon.
