06/06/16

 

July 14 – Ski Apache - Sierra Blanca – Ruidoso, New Mexico residents Riker Davis and his son, Tucker, are long time hang glider and paraglider pilots, respectively, and have worked tirelessly for years to develop this matchless area for hang gliding and paragliding.

Riker, a former employee of Ski Apache, and Justin Rowland, Director of Operations of the resort, teamed up to open this popular ski attraction to commercial tandem flights for visitors.  This is where Had Robinson, a USHPA advanced and tandem instructor came in to the mix:  Someone was needed who could legally give tandem flights.  What now has to happen is the area must be flown solo countless times by experienced pilots in order to learn the air with certainty.  This is necessary for the complete safety of passengers who want to enjoy the skies near the tallest mountain in the southwest.  Accordingly, Tucker and Had dove right in that Friday morning to fly Ski Apache.  We started first with launching from the top of the Capitan Triple Chair #4 lift and landing in the main parking lot at the base of the ski area.  We will also begin flying from the very top of the ski area near Apache Bowl Triple Chair #6 lift (over 11,000' MSL).

Tucker made the first paraglider flight ever inside Ski Apache and Had made the second.  HD YouTube videos of both flights can be viewed here (Tucker) and here (Had).  It looks easy but this is launching and landing in a deep canyon in the highest mountains of south central New Mexico.  Prevailing winds here are a danger as we must rely on wind currents driven by the sun's convective heat hitting the east slopes of the range.  Everyone hopes that Tucker will get his commercial tandem instructor certification soon.  We need more capable pilots!

BTW, if you need to rent a cabin in the Ruidoso area, reserve a ski package, and the like, Riker (owner of Condotel Corp) is your man.  Contact him at: 800-545-9017 or visit his website www.ruidosoreservations.com.

Below, Tucker and his dad, Riker, contemplate the flight from what would be the highest accessible launch site in the southwest – over 11,000' MSL.  The 12,000' Sierra Blanca looms in the background.  It was cold up there – 52 degrees.  (For my fellow residents in and near El Paso, does "cold" sound like an odd word to you in the middle of July?)

Looking northeast from the top of Apache Bowl to Buck Mountain (10,700').  Now wouldn't this be a sled ride to the bottom – over 1.5 miles of vertical drop?  The Capitan Mountains are visible in the far distance.  Our challenge in flying the Bowl is to have enough good lift to clear the saddle (white arrow) on our way down to the LZ (the base of the ski area).  This base is not visible here as it lies in a deep canyon beyond the ridge visible here going from left to right.

Tucker laying out his glider for the historic flight from northeast/east facing slope at the top of the Capitan lift.  Remember that winds aloft here are from the southwest.  How will they affect the lower air currents that result from convection and are from the east?  Will there be ripping turbulence or rotor when we launch? Or?  This is why pioneering site work can be dicey.  Did I forget something???  In other words, it takes manhood to do this kind of stuff....

The air was buoyant right off launch and Tucker got right up.  Predictably, the dark parking lot was throwing off significant thermals.  Had Robinson launched to the right (see the video above) and was able to get even more lift as he entered a thermal that followed along the top of the trees to the right.  However, today was not the day to thermal out of the ski area – next time, maybe.

In a thermic area like this parking lot, winds are light and variable – if you don't get popped by a powerful thermal coming off the hot asphalt.  Tucker was able to fly all the way to the east (right) end of the parking lot and all the way back to the west end and land – going slightly uphill.  This is because cold air is still pouring down the sides of these mountains nearly all morning.  When Had landed a half hour later, air was still coming down the mountain.  His chosen LZ was sloping down the mountain and he landed downwind – it was quite a run.  But we are learning about the site and where a safe tandem could be flown and where to land.  Again, pioneering sites can be dicey.

The happy pilots Tucker and Had (in hat) posing at the entrance to Ski Apache (currently closed, however, because of the drought and fire danger.  It will open soon because we had good rains recently.  WE HAD FAR MORE FUN DOING THIS THAN ANY NORMAL PERSON IS ALLOWED TO HAVE.  Imagine getting the privilege of flying one of the highest spots in the southwest?

Windy Point Launch (10,050' MSL)  – The green arrows point to launch area. The left arrow has a rugged launch but is right on the road to Ski Apache.  The right arrow points to a huge grassy saddle for an easy launch but you have to hike about 10 minutes to get there.  The photo was taken in the LZ which is an easy 2:1 sled ride from launch (2,000' above the LZ).  The launch (east of the peak of Buck Mountain) could be a prime site for paragliding in New Mexico.  As it faces east, it is protected from the stronger and higher winds aloft by the peaks west of it.  Like the air at Ski Apache, it relies on convection from the sun to get an upslope breeze.  Once pilots get up, they can thermal away to ? or ride the ridge lift out in front of launch.  The Mescalero Apache Indian Tribe has generously offered their help in making this entire area flyable.  We are very thankful for their willingness to see hang gliders and paragliders above the Reservation.

We hope you enjoyed the show!  Remember:  Fly more, work less.
