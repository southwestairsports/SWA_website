July 21 – Lookout Mountain (11,542'), Ruidoso, NM – First Paragliding Flights from the Summit

This was the big day for Justin Rowland (Ski Apache Director of Operations), Riker Davis, son Tucker, Fred Pavlovic, & Had Robinson.  Tucker & Had would have the opportunity to be the first pilots to launch from Lookout Mountain (the top of Apache Bowl).  Lookout is just west of the highest peak in the southwest, Sierra Blanca (12,005').  Justin, Riker, & Fred assisted the pilots.

The east facing slopes of these mountains heat up from the sun and this causes the air above them to warm and rise, creating an easterly flow up the mountains during the morning hours.  It is this convective flow that gives us the lift we hope to fly in.  The challenge:  Is there enough lift off launch to clear the north rim of the Bowl in order to make the LZ at the base of the ski area?  If there is not, the pilot must ditch somewhere out in front – hopefully not in the trees.  How will the winds aloft affect our flights?  A four minute YouTube video of the launch, flight, and landing can be seen here.

Tucker, the first paragliding pilot to ever fly from the top, waits for the right moment to inflate his glider and fly away.

The view north from the summit of Lookout Mountain – this is what the pilot sees when launching.  Just above the wind-streamer in the foreground is the ridge that pilots must clear in order to make the LZ.  Buck Mountain is in the distance and the Capitan Mountains in the far distance.

Fred Pavlovic (L) and Riker Davis (R) near launch at the summit.  Assistance at launch is always valuable to pilots, especially when they are exploring new air, such as here.  Just behind the summit, the winds aloft were west.  If the convective flow on the east side stops, you are flying in rotor.  When might this occur?  Questions like this are present in the mind of the pilot when flying in active air.

Tucker Davis gathering up his wing in the LZ after his historic flight from Lookout Mountain.  All went well!  Tucker won the wager to land closest to the orange cone in the LZ – the other pilot has to buy the celebratory brew.

A piece of history that Riker Davis found in the tall grass near the summit of Lookout Mountain.
