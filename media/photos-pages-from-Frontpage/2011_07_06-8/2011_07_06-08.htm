06/06/16

 

July 6-8 – Aden Volcanic Field – Bill Cummings and yours truly (Had Robinson) managed to get away for a few days to fly the beauty and vastness of the Field which is located in south central New Mexico.  We stayed near an old cinder quarry approximately 1.5 miles WNW of Aden Mountain (32.141260°  -107.127264°).  Most everything has been brown for at least 10 months in the following photos but we are entering the monsoon season and we should have some moisture which will make the entire area briefly green.

An evening flight over the Field.  In the far distance (18 miles away to the south) are the three peaks of Mt. Riley, Cox, and No-Name.  To their left is the (just visible) East Potrillo Mountain chain.
 

Our home for three days.  This cone was heavily mined for its lava cinders (red and black) decades ago.  The sharp east face provided a shady refuge from the sun late in the day.  I was able to park my RV trailer close to the base of it.  In the late of the day, dust devils would tear through here, even throwing my paramotor so hard against my back window that it broke it!

Despite the drought, there are creatures that can obviously live on the dry and sparse vegetation that is out there, including this Jack Rabbit.  I was able to get amazingly close to this one.  Perhaps he had never before seen a human being "What's this biped thing?  Not a hawk, coyote, or eagle.  Maybe it's related to cattle?"  The lava of the field is plainly visible on the ground.  In the three days we were there, we never saw or heard a coyote.

Bill surveys the east side of the Aden Hills for a possible training area for paragliding and hang gliding.  The gentle slope goes down for a 1/4 mile or more with no obstacles at the bottom.  The Hills are a high point in the area and are populated with microwave relay stations.

An ancient (but well made) paved road that went west to the cinder cone we stayed at.

The vast region is a delight to fly over, especially 10' off the ground like a cruise missile.  We always ask ourselves, "Is this earth???"

Unpopulated and remote areas of the southwest are often used as corridors to smuggle both people and drugs from Mexico.  Of course, our activities drew a lot of attention from the ever-vigilant U.S. Border Patrol.  This sharp-eyed agent saw me pop up into the air and then disappear down low but followed me for miles.  The cartels use ultralights to transport their loads north so our presence had to be carefully investigated.  As we do not usually fly in this sector of the southwest, we were unknown.  We know have a special number that we call if we plan to operate in this area.  The Patrol has a dangerous and tough job and, as responsible citizens, we need to help them.  I handed out many business cards to the agents as PPG would be an ideal way to inexpensively and easily patrol many square miles of the mountainous desert.  The cost of operation of a foot launched system would be about the same as an ATV.

Another group of agents paid us a visit.  These guys get paid to ride ATV's! The next step:  Get paid to fly PPG.

The drought is tough on even the insects.  Honey bees quickly smelled the occasional splash of water we made so we set out a bowl of water for them.  These bees consumed over a pint every hour!  The hive must have been close to here.  The nearest other water is at the Johnson Ranch about 5 miles SE of us – a long way for a bee to travel.  We put the empty soda can out to help them find the water.  Bees can see color and we didn't want them exploring out camp site too much....

Another one of the many cones in the area.  I was able to ridge soar this one late in the day.  The air was coming in at about 11 and provided just enough lift to stay up without power.
 

The Southern Pacific railroad has their main east-west track just east of the Field.  It's a lonely stretch of track.

The Johnson Ranch – we didn't count but 10 cattle in the area as the drought has been severe.  Ranches are sparse out here.  It is a lonely existence to be sure but peaceful.  It is located about 13 miles north of Riley, Cox, and No-Name which can be seen to the south.

Hope you enjoyed the show!
