Woods Hole & the Elizabeth Islands, Massachusetts

launch/LZ N41.52935° W70.67043° MSL 10'; PPG ONLY -- ADVANCED SKILLS REQUIRED

Great Harbor, Penzance Point, and the Elizabeth Islands stretching west into the distance

The area is a magical mix of of ocean and land.  It is relatively un-crowded.  The Elizabeth Islands are privately owned and the trustees have graciously allowed a few areas for public use, including Tarpaulin Cove, Kettle Cove, Bull Island, the Weepecket Islands, and West Beach.  Cuttyhunk Island has a small village and was once home to U-Boat lookout stations during WWII.  Pilots will find the isolated Tarpaulin Cove the easiest to land/launch from.

Launch, LZ and Bailout

The Woods Hole ballpark is the only flat and somewhat clear pilots can use.   The trees cause turbulence and rotor, especially in high winds.  Though this is not particularly dangerous, pilots need to ensure that they launch at trim speed and not do any turns or maneuvers until well away from the terrain.  The air can be all over the place....

 Here is a view of the ballpark looking east.

If the ballpark has activity, it is easy to land in the bailout but requires walking through Poison Ivy where it connects with the ballpark.  The owners of the marsh have graciously given pilots permission to land but they first must identify themselves.  Please contact us for the details.

If winds are high, the bailout is a great place to ditch as the ground is essentially matted vegetation and very soft.  Both areas are prone to turbulence and rotor because of trees.  It is not a good idea to launch if winds are more than 15 anywhere.  The Buzzards Bay Tower and Provincetown stations are the most important to check before flying as they are relatively unaffected by land and give the wind direction and speed pilots will experience when flying near the surface of the ocean.  Generally, winds pick up in the afternoon and can be 15-20 knots.  Always check the forecasts, especially the NWS hourly, for this region.

Getting caught in the air when it is blowing 25, even though smooth, can be dangerous for landing.  Up is west in the photo below.

Pilots must give way to all other users of the ballpark.  Quickly leave the area once you are in the air.  The best bailout LZ is the surrounding marsh which is, essentially, like landing in the water because of the great thickness of the marsh vegetation.  The downside is that there is a lot of turbulence and rotor in the marsh area but it is still safer to have a hard landing there than risk drowning, going in the trees, or hitting the ground.  Once in the marsh, it is a relatively easy walk out, a pilot may have to cross a narrow canal or two.

Hadley Harbor is surrounded by various islands

HAZARDS

Getting blown out over the ocean could be a disaster if a pilot has to land in the water.  It is mandatory to have adequate flotation devices, including an inflatable-type life preserver and equipment like the Powerfloat.  Drowning is the #1 cause of death for PPG pilots and is so easily avoidable.

Pilots should carry a waterproof strobe, as having to ditch in the water is always a possibility.  Ditching in the surf while attached to your glider would probably be fatal.  Weather on Cape Cod can be gusty and very windy with little warning.  Never fly where there is not a reasonable glide to land as ditching in the ocean would be expensive and possibly dangerous if you are far from land.

As is true everywhere on planet earth, the Jet Stream can make the air gusty so it is a good idea to stay on the ground when it is near or overhead.

WARNING

This air space is closely watched by AMOC as VIP's frequently visit the area.  All pilots need to check for TFR's and give all inhabited areas wide berth, especially near Woods Hole.  Thankfully, the shoreline of the Elizabeth Islands is largely uninhabited so flying low and slow there will not bother anyone.
